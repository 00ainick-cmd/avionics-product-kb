# Garmin GNC 255 Nav/Comm

**Manufacturer:** Garmin
**Category:** Comm Radios / Nav/Comm
**Price Range:** $5,500--$8,000 (installed)
**STC Coverage:** Broad -- most Part 23 GA single and twin-engine aircraft
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## What It Is

The Garmin GNC 255 is a panel-mounted VHF Nav/Comm radio that combines a full VOR/LOC/Glideslope navigation receiver with a VHF communications transceiver in a single standard radio stack box. It is the default navcomm replacement in general aviation shops today, covering the enormous installed base of aging King KX 155s, KX 170Bs, Narco MK-12D units, and Garmin's own legacy SL30. Available in two versions: the GNC 255A with a 10-watt comm transmitter and the GNC 255B with a 16-watt transmitter.

- **For the Entry-Level Tech:** A navcomm is two radios in one box. The "comm" side lets the pilot talk to ATC and other aircraft on VHF frequencies (118.000--136.975 MHz). The "nav" side receives VOR and ILS navigation signals from ground stations, which the pilot uses to navigate airways and fly instrument approaches. The GNC 255 replaces the old, heavy analog radios that dominated GA panels for 30+ years.
- **Why It Still Matters:** Even in a GPS-dominant world, the FAA still requires conventional VOR/ILS navigation capability as a backup for most IFR-equipped aircraft. GPS signals can be jammed or disrupted. A working navcomm is insurance -- and the GNC 255 is the most widely supported policy on the market.

## How It Works

The GNC 255 operates on two independent receiver/transmitter paths inside the same chassis:

- **Comm Section:** Standard VHF AM transceiver covering 118.000--136.975 MHz. Supports 8.33 kHz channel spacing, which is the European requirement and is increasingly relevant for aircraft that cross the Atlantic or operate in ICAO regions outside the US. Active and standby frequency display with a flip-flop button. The 255A outputs 10 watts; the 255B outputs 16 watts, which matters for aircraft that routinely operate at distance from ground stations or in mountainous terrain where signal margin counts.
- **Nav Section:** VOR/Localizer/Glideslope receiver. The nav receiver outputs composite analog deviation signals (VOR radial deviation and LOC/GS deviation) that drive external course deviation indicators (CDIs), horizontal situation indicators (HSIs), and autopilot nav couplers. It will drive a King KI 209 CDI, a KI 525A HSI, a Garmin GI 106A CDI, the GI 275 electronic HSI, or virtually any standard CDI/HSI on the market. The nav section also provides an audio ident signal so the pilot can aurally confirm the VOR station.

The front panel features a bright LED backlit LCD display with active and standby frequencies for both comm and nav, plus OBS (omni bearing selector) adjustment for the nav side. The display is legible in direct sunlight -- a significant improvement over the dim incandescent-lit displays on the KX 155s it typically replaces.

## Installation Considerations

The GNC 255 occupies a standard 6.25-inch-wide radio stack slot at 1.65 inches tall -- the same footprint as the radios it replaces. In many aircraft, the swap is close to plug-and-play, but "close" is the operative word.

- **Wiring:** The GNC 255 uses a different rear connector and pinout than King or Narco units. Expect new wiring harnesses. If the existing harness is old and brittle, the shop should replace it entirely rather than splicing adapters. The performance of the new radio is only as good as the wiring behind it.
- **Antenna:** Evaluate the existing comm and nav antennas before committing. A corroded or sun-damaged fiberglass comm antenna will degrade transmit range regardless of how new the radio is. VOR/LOC antennas (typically a V-dipole in the tail) should be checked for continuity and proper impedance. Budget $200--$500 per antenna if replacements are needed.
- **CDI Compatibility:** The nav output is standard composite deviation signals, so it connects directly to most CDIs and HSIs. If the aircraft has a Garmin GI 275 configured as an HSI, the interface is straightforward analog input. If connecting to an older King KI 209 CDI, verify the converter flag circuitry works correctly -- some legacy CDI/GNC combinations have flag driver quirks that require an inline resistor change.
- **Autopilot Interface:** The GNC 255 outputs nav deviation signals that can drive autopilot nav couplers directly. No GAD 29 adapter is required when connecting to Garmin autopilots for nav coupling -- the 255 wires direct. This is a notable cost and complexity savings compared to some GPS navigator-to-autopilot interfaces.
- **Typical Downtime:** 1--3 days for a straightforward one-for-one replacement where the existing wiring is in acceptable condition. Add time if antennas need replacing or if the shop discovers corroded connectors behind the panel.

## Flight / Bench Performance

The comm transmitter is clean and reliable. Audio quality through the GNC 255 is noticeably better than the legacy King and Narco units it replaces, partly because the audio processing is modern digital and partly because those old radios have 30+ years of component drift working against them.

The nav receiver tracks VOR radials and ILS localizer/glideslope signals with the accuracy you would expect from a current-production TSO'd unit. Station identification is positive, flag behavior is correct, and the deviation outputs are stable. The glideslope receiver handles both standard 90/150 Hz modulation frequencies without issue.

Frequency tuning via the concentric knobs is positive and predictable. The flip-flop button for swapping active and standby is responsive. There is no touchscreen -- this is a knobs-and-buttons radio, which is exactly what you want in turbulence or when wearing gloves.

One practical note: the 8.33 kHz spacing mode is selectable. In the US, 25 kHz spacing is standard and 8.33 is unnecessary. But if the aircraft ever transits to Europe or certain other ICAO regions, the capability is already there. No hardware change, no software update, no return to the shop.

## Related Products

- **Trig TX56A/TX57:** Navcomm with a thinner 1.3-inch form factor. Includes a built-in intercom and voice activation. Approximately $4,175. Smaller North American dealer network.
- **Honeywell KX 200:** Direct drop-in replacement for the KX 155 that reuses the existing wiring harness. Approximately $5,100. Single-vendor solution with a narrower support base.
- **Garmin GNC 355:** Adds built-in GPS navigator with LPV approach capability to the navcomm package. Approximately $7,000 box price. Eliminates the need for a separate GPS navigator in simple IFR panels.
- **Legacy King KX 155A / KX 170B:** No longer in production. Used units available for $1,500–$2,500. Parts availability is declining, and overhaul costs can exceed the value of the radio.

## Price & Value

- **Box Price (GNC 255A, 10W):** approximately $4,500
- **Box Price (GNC 255B, 16W):** approximately $5,500
- **Installation Labor:** $1,000--$2,500 depending on wiring condition, antenna needs, and CDI interface work
- **Total Installed:** $5,500--$8,000

Is it worth it? If the aircraft has a dead or dying KX 155, the GNC 255 is the path of least resistance. It is the most commonly installed navcomm replacement in GA, which means every shop knows how to install it, every display manufacturer supports it, and parts availability is not a concern. The 16-watt B model is worth the upcharge for aircraft that operate in the backcountry or at airports far from major metro areas where comm range matters.

For aircraft that already have a modern GPS navigator handling all nav duties, consider whether you actually need the nav section at all. If the answer is "IFR backup only," the GNC 255 delivers. If the answer is "I never use VOR anymore," a comm-only radio like the GTR 225 saves money.

## Summary

The GNC 255 is a VHF Nav/Comm combining VOR/LOC/GS receiver with VHF communications transceiver. Key characteristics:
- Standard 6.25-inch radio stack form factor. Replaces KX 155, KX 170B, Narco MK-12D, and Garmin SL30.
- Available in 10W (255A) and 16W (255B) comm transmitter versions.
- 8.33 kHz channel spacing is built in for European/ICAO compliance.
- Nav deviation outputs drive standard CDIs, HSIs, and autopilot nav couplers.
- For aircraft where GPS handles all navigation and no VOR/ILS backup is needed, a comm-only radio (GTR 225, Trig TX56A) costs less.
- For aircraft needing GPS approach capability from the radio stack, the GNC 355 integrates a GPS navigator.

## Key Technical Facts

- Standard 6.25" × 1.65" radio stack slot. Different rear connector and pinout than King/Narco units — new wiring harness required.
- The 16W 255B model provides additional transmit margin for operations in mountainous terrain or at distance from ground stations.
- VOR/LOC/GS nav deviation outputs are standard composite analog — compatible with most CDIs, HSIs, and autopilot nav couplers including GI 275, KI 209, KI 525A, and GI 106A.
- No GAD 29 adapter required for autopilot nav coupling when connecting to Garmin autopilots.
- Antenna condition should be evaluated during installation — a corroded comm or nav antenna will negate the performance benefit of a new radio.
- Typical downtime: 1–3 days for one-for-one replacement.
