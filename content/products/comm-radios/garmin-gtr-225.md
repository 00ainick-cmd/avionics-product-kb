# Garmin GTR 225 Comm Radio

**Manufacturer:** Garmin
**Category:** Comm Radios
**Price Range:** $3,500--$5,000 (installed)
**STC Coverage:** Broad -- most Part 23 GA single and twin-engine aircraft
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## What It Is

The Garmin GTR 225 is a panel-mounted VHF communications transceiver -- comm only, no navigation receiver. It transmits and receives on the standard aviation VHF band (118.000--136.975 MHz) at 10 watts, with 8.33 kHz channel spacing support, and fits a standard radio stack slot. It exists for aircraft that do not need another VOR/ILS nav source because a GPS navigator is already handling all navigation duties.

- **For the Entry-Level Tech:** This is a talk-and-listen radio with no navigation capability. The pilot uses it to communicate with ATC, ground control, approach, tower, CTAF, and Flight Service. Unlike a navcomm (which combines comm and nav in one box), the GTR 225 is purely a communications transceiver. There is no VOR receiver, no ILS, no glideslope -- just comms.
- **Why It Exists:** Modern GA panels increasingly rely on GPS navigators (GTN 750Xi, GTN 650Xi, IFD550) for all navigation. These navigators already contain their own VOR/ILS receivers. A second VOR/ILS receiver in the stack is redundant weight and cost. The GTR 225 gives the pilot reliable comm capability without paying for a nav receiver that would sit unused.

## How It Works

The GTR 225 is a straightforward VHF AM transceiver. It operates on the aviation comm band from 118.000 to 136.975 MHz with selectable 25 kHz or 8.33 kHz channel spacing.

- **Transmitter:** 10-watt output. Adequate for typical GA operations -- line-of-sight comm range at altitude is primarily a function of antenna height above terrain, and 10 watts is the standard power level for GA comm radios. For aircraft that routinely need extra transmit margin, consider the GNC 255B (16W) navcomm or the Trig TY97A (16W) instead.
- **Display:** Bright LED backlit LCD showing active and standby frequencies simultaneously. Flip-flop button swaps active and standby. The display is a meaningful upgrade from the dim, aging displays on whatever legacy radio it is replacing.
- **Tuning:** Concentric knobs for MHz and kHz tuning. Tactile, positive clicks. No touchscreen.
- **Audio:** The GTR 225 connects to the aircraft audio panel via standard audio lines. Sidetone, squelch, and volume are adjustable from the front panel.

There is no GPS integration, no Bluetooth, and no built-in intercom on the base GTR 225. It does one thing and does it well. For Bluetooth connectivity or 3D audio processing, Garmin offers the GTR 200B at a lower price point, which is a newer design with those features. The GTR 225 is the legacy-form-factor workhorse.

## Installation Considerations

The GTR 225 occupies a standard 6.25-inch-wide radio stack slot at 1.35 inches tall -- slightly shorter than the GNC 255 (1.65 inches). It fits anywhere a legacy comm radio lived.

- **Wiring:** Simpler than a navcomm installation because there are no nav deviation outputs to route to CDIs, HSIs, or autopilots. The connections are: antenna coax, audio lines to the audio panel, mic input, PTT (push-to-talk), and power/ground. If replacing a navcomm with a comm-only radio, the shop will need to cap or remove the now-unused nav wiring -- or leave it in place if a future navcomm is possible.
- **Antenna:** Uses a standard VHF comm antenna. If the aircraft currently has a working comm antenna, verify its condition (corrosion, antistatic coating, connector integrity) and reuse it. If adding the GTR 225 as a second comm radio where only one existed before, a second comm antenna is required -- typically a belly-mounted blade antenna. Budget $200--$500 for the antenna plus installation labor.
- **Audio Panel Requirement:** If this is the aircraft's second comm radio, an audio panel is necessary to manage comm selection (Comm 1 vs. Comm 2 transmit and receive). Most IFR-equipped aircraft already have one. If the aircraft has no audio panel, factor in the cost of adding one -- a Garmin GMA 345 runs approximately $2,300 installed.
- **Typical Downtime:** 1--2 days. Comm-only installations are among the simplest radio jobs in the shop. No nav wiring, no CDI interface, no autopilot coupling to verify. If the antenna and audio panel are already in place, an experienced shop can have the aircraft back on the ramp in a day.

## Flight / Bench Performance

The GTR 225 transmits clean audio and receives with good sensitivity. Squelch behavior is predictable. Frequency switching is fast. There is nothing remarkable to report, which is exactly the point -- a comm radio should be transparent. You key the mic, you talk, the other end hears you clearly. The GTR 225 does that.

The LED display is significantly brighter and more legible than the incandescent-lit displays on legacy King and Narco comm radios. In direct afternoon sun through a Cessna windshield, the frequencies are still readable without squinting.

Build quality is consistent with Garmin's current panel-mount line. The unit weighs under two pounds. The concentric tuning knobs have positive detent clicks with no slop.

## Related Products

- **Trig TX56A:** Comm-only at approximately $1,865 (10W). Thinner at 1.3 inches. Includes 30-second audio replay, Dual Watch mode, and USB memory for custom frequency databases. Smaller North American support network.
- **Icom IC-A220:** 8W TSO'd comm with OLED display and 180-degree viewing angle. Competitively priced. Lower transmit power may be a consideration for some operations.
- **Garmin GTR 200B:** Garmin's newer, lower-cost comm at approximately $1,199–$1,599. Includes Bluetooth audio streaming and 3D audio processing.
- **Garmin GNC 255A:** Adds a VOR/ILS nav receiver for approximately $1,800 more. Appropriate when VOR/ILS backup is needed for IFR operations or resale value.
- **Becker AR-6201/AR-6203:** Space-saving European designs. The AR-6201 fits a 2.25-inch instrument cutout for tight or unconventional panels.

## Price & Value

- **Box Price:** approximately $2,695
- **Installation Labor:** $800--$2,000 depending on antenna needs and wiring condition
- **Total Installed:** $3,500--$5,000

The GTR 225 is a sensible purchase when the aircraft already has a GPS navigator with its own VOR/ILS receiver and the pilot simply needs reliable comm capability in the second radio stack position. It avoids paying for a nav receiver that would duplicate what the GPS navigator already provides.

The value proposition weakens if you compare it directly against the GTR 200B, which is Garmin's newer comm radio design at a lower price with Bluetooth and 3D audio. The GTR 225 is the older, more established product. Some shops prefer it for consistency with existing installs, and some pilots prefer the knob ergonomics. But for a clean-sheet installation in a panel that does not already have a GTR 225, ask the shop to quote both the GTR 225 and GTR 200B.

If there is any uncertainty about whether the aircraft needs VOR/ILS navigation capability -- for IFR operations, for resale, or for regulatory changes -- spend the extra money and install the GNC 255 navcomm instead. Removing a comm radio later to install a navcomm means paying for labor twice.

## Summary

The GTR 225 is a comm-only VHF transceiver for aircraft where GPS navigation has made a second VOR/ILS receiver unnecessary. Key characteristics:
- 10W VHF comm transceiver covering 118.000–136.975 MHz with 8.33 kHz spacing.
- No VOR/ILS navigation receiver — appropriate when a GPS navigator already provides nav capability.
- Concentric knob tuning, no touchscreen. Under 2 lbs.
- Newer Garmin GTR 200B offers Bluetooth and 3D audio at a lower price point. Compare both before committing to a new installation.
- If there is any uncertainty about VOR/ILS need, the GNC 255 navcomm avoids paying for labor twice.

## Key Technical Facts

- Standard 6.25" × 1.35" radio stack slot. Slightly shorter than the GNC 255.
- Comm-only wiring is simpler than navcomm: antenna coax, audio lines, mic input, PTT, and power/ground. No nav deviation outputs to route.
- If replacing a navcomm with a comm-only unit, existing nav wiring must be capped or removed.
- A second comm radio requires an audio panel for comm selection.
- Typical downtime: 1–2 days. One of the simplest radio installations in the shop.
