# Autopilot Pre-Buy: How to Avoid Clunkers

**Source:** Aviation Consumer
**Author:** Larry Anglisano
**Date:** February 2008
**URL:** https://aviationconsumer.com/uncategorized/autopilot-pre-buy-how-to-avoid-clunkers/
**Category:** Autopilots / Pre-Buy / Shop Business
**Products covered:** BendixKing KFC200, KFC225, KAP140, KI256, S-TEC 55X, 60-2, Bendix M4D, FCS810, Century, Brittain, ARC/Cessna 800-series

---

This article addresses a critical concern for used aircraft buyers: evaluating autopilot systems before purchase. Autopilots are major systems whose replacement costs can significantly exceed engine replacement expenses, making thorough pre-buy inspection essential.

## Key Inspection Recommendations

During aircraft evaluation, buyers should review logbook entries for autopilot repairs, conduct test flights with coupled approaches, watch for roll/pitch oscillations and course deviations, and request detailed repair documentation. Pages and pages of logged autopilot repair should raise a red flag.

## Systems to Avoid

Problematic systems include: Bendix M4D (servo costs ~$9,000 each), Bendix FCS810-series (scarce parts), Pneumatic Brittain models (outdated design), Early ARC/Cessna 800-series (expensive, complex), and older Century systems.

## Acceptable Systems

BendixKing KFC200 is attitude-based with good support despite age. KFC225 is a modern digital system with solid performance. KAP140 is rate-based with decent capabilities in newer Cessnas.

S-TEC systems have modular "building block" design allowing upgrades. The 55X and 60-2 offer full features with an affordable upgrade path.

## Cost Considerations

Replacement parts are increasingly scarce for older systems. Component examples: $3,600 on exchange for a KI256 gyro, with thousands more for attitude director indicator replacement.

## Advice

Know what you are buying before purchasing a used aircraft. Autopilot upgrades are expensive and may result in fewer features than the original system. Rate-based systems are simpler and less expensive to maintain than attitude-based systems.
