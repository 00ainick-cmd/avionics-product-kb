# Garmin GMA 345

**Manufacturer:** Garmin
**Category:** Audio Panels
**Price Range:** $2,500–$4,000 (installed)
**STC Coverage:** Broad AML-STC covering most Part 23 piston singles and light twins; essentially any GA aircraft with a standard radio stack.
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## What It Is

The Garmin GMA 345 is a Bluetooth-enabled audio panel with integrated stereo intercom that replaces the venerable GMA 340 — a panel that has been sitting in more GA radio stacks than probably any other audio panel in history. It handles all audio routing between the pilot's headsets, comm radios, nav receivers, and external audio sources, and it does it with a handful of genuinely useful modern features baked into the same form factor the 340 occupied.

- **For the Entry-Level Tech (Non-Pilot):** This is the central switchboard for everything the pilot hears and says. ATC calls, intercom conversations with passengers, music from a phone, marker beacon tones — all of it flows through this box. The GMA 345 decides what gets routed where and at what volume.
- **Why Pilots Want It:** Because their 20-year-old GMA 340 still works fine but has no Bluetooth, no clearance recorder, and no way to spatially separate radio calls. The 345 adds all of that for under $2,000 box price and slides into the same wiring harness.

## How It Works

The GMA 345 manages audio for up to two comm radios, two nav receivers, a telephone input, and two wired auxiliary music inputs, plus Bluetooth.

- **3D Audio:** This is the headline feature. The GMA 345 uses phase-shifting to create the perception that different audio sources are coming from different physical locations in the headset. Comm 1 sounds like it originates from your left, Comm 2 from your right, intercom from straight ahead. The result is that the brain can separate overlapping audio without conscious effort. It requires stereo headsets and stereo jacks to function — monaural headsets plugged into stereo jacks will short the right channel to ground and you will get left-channel audio in both ears. Not damaging, but it kills the spatial effect entirely.
- **Avionics Squelch (ASQ):** Garmin's auto-squelch processing that strips the background hiss from VHF comm audio. It is effective and eliminates the constant squelch-knob fiddling, though shops that have compared it directly to PS Engineering's IntelliVox sometimes give the nod to PS Engineering for rejecting aggressive noise sources like an open storm window.
- **Clearance Recorder:** A 60-second digital loop that captures the last ATC transmission. If you miss a rapid-fire clearance readback, you push the PLAY button and hear it again. Simple. Useful. Should have been standard in audio panels a decade ago.
- **Split Comm:** Pilot and copilot can independently monitor and transmit on different comm frequencies. Handy for IFR work where one pilot monitors approach while the other handles ground or ATIS.
- **Bluetooth:** Pairs with one device at a time via A2DP for streaming audio and HFP for phone calls. Remembers up to 10 paired devices. There is also a USB Type-A charging port on the faceplate rated at 2.1 amps — enough to keep an iPad alive.
- **Integrated Marker Beacon Receiver:** Built into the unit. One less separate box in the stack.

## Installation Considerations

- **The GMA 340 Swap:** This is where the GMA 345 earns its keep from a shop perspective. If the aircraft currently has a GMA 340, the 345 is designed as a direct slide-in replacement using the same wiring harness and connector pinout. Pull the old panel out of its tray, slide the new one in, power up, configure, done. This is a 1-day job at most shops, and the labor bill reflects it.
- **Non-Garmin Legacy Replacement:** If the aircraft has an older King KMA 24, a PS Engineering PMA 6000-series, or some other legacy audio panel, the install becomes a standard audio panel swap. The shop will need to re-pin the connector and potentially run new wiring to headset jacks. This is not catastrophic — audio panel wiring is well-documented and straightforward — but it adds labor. Budget for 1.5 to 2 days.
- **Form Factor:** Standard 6.3 x 1.33 x 8.09 inches, weighing 1.78 pounds. Fits a standard radio stack slot with no panel cutting required.
- **Headset Jack Consideration:** If the aircraft currently has monaural headset jacks and you want the customer to benefit from 3D Audio, you will need to swap the jacks to stereo. This is relatively minor work but it is an easy item to miss on the quote.
- **Diagnostics:** An extended press of the PLAY button initiates a built-in diagnostic mode that helps troubleshoot audio routing issues. Useful for verifying the install and for future squawk chasing.

## Flight / Bench Performance

The audio quality is a noticeable step up from the GMA 340. The ASQ processing cleans up comm audio effectively, and the 3D Audio spatial separation is not a gimmick — it is a genuine improvement when monitoring two frequencies simultaneously. In a busy terminal environment, having Comm 1 perceived as coming from one direction and Comm 2 from another measurably reduces the cognitive load of parsing overlapping transmissions.

Bluetooth audio quality is adequate for music and phone calls. It is not audiophile-grade, but nobody is buying an audio panel for its Bose credentials. The clearance recorder works as advertised and has a clean playback quality.

The push-button controls with green annunciators and photocell-driven brightness control carry over from the GMA 340 — proven, tactile, and readable across lighting conditions. Dual rotary knobs for pilot and copilot/passenger volume are intuitive and require no fumbling.

## Price & Value

- **Box price:** Approximately $1,895
- **GMA 340 slide-in replacement (installed):** $2,500–$3,000 — mostly the cost of the unit plus a few hours of labor and a test flight
- **Full legacy panel replacement (installed):** $3,000–$4,000 — additional labor for rewiring and connector re-pinning

The GMA 345 hits the sweet spot of features versus price for most GA owners. For roughly $2,500 installed as a 340 replacement, the customer gets Bluetooth, 3D Audio, a clearance recorder, and auto-squelch in a box that drops into their existing wiring. That is a lot of capability for a modest outlay. The PMA 450B is objectively better in several areas, but it costs $1,400 more just for the hardware — a tough sell when the 345 covers 90% of what most pilots actually need.

## Summary

The GMA 345 is a Bluetooth-enabled audio panel with integrated intercom. Key characteristics:
- Designed as a direct slide-in replacement for the GMA 340 using the same wiring harness and connector pinout.
- 3D Audio uses phase-shifting to spatially separate audio sources in stereo headsets.
- 60-second clearance recorder captures the last ATC transmission for playback.
- Split Comm allows pilot and copilot to independently monitor and transmit on separate COM frequencies.
- The PS Engineering PMA 450B offers a different approach to spatial audio (IntelliAudio) and features dual Bluetooth modules and a longer clearance recorder at a higher price point.
- Requires stereo headsets for 3D Audio functionality. Monaural headsets will receive left-channel audio only.

## Key Technical Facts

- Direct slide-in replacement for the GMA 340 — same tray, same connector pinout. Typically a 1-day installation.
- 3D Audio spatially separates COM 1 (left), COM 2 (right), and intercom (center) to reduce cognitive load during simultaneous monitoring.
- 60-second clearance recorder with push-button playback.
- Bluetooth A2DP for audio streaming and HFP for phone calls. Remembers up to 10 paired devices. USB Type-A charging port (2.1A) on the faceplate.
- Integrated marker beacon receiver eliminates the need for a separate box.
- When replacing non-Garmin legacy audio panels (KMA 24, PMA 6000-series), the shop will need to re-pin the connector and potentially run new wiring to headset jacks. Budget for 1.5 to 2 days.

## Common Squawks & Troubleshooting

### Bluetooth Not Discoverable / Audio Dropout
- **Symptom:** The GMA 345 does not appear in the device's Bluetooth pairing list, or after a successful pairing, Bluetooth audio intermittently cuts out or is not heard through the headsets.
- **Root Cause:** A software bug in pre-2.40 firmware caused intermittent Bluetooth discovery failures and audio routing issues.
- **Resolution:** Update to **System Software Version 2.40** per **Garmin Service Bulletin 24087 Rev A** (December 20, 2024). This update fixed the Bluetooth pairing visibility issue and the intermittent audio dropout. Compliance recommended at next maintenance interval. Warranty reimbursable for eligible units through May 31, 2025.
- **Source:** Garmin SB 24087 Rev A

### Monitored COM Auto-Muting (Feature, Not a Bug)
- **Symptom:** When the pilot is transmitting or receiving on their selected COM radio, the volume of the *other* (deselected/monitored) COM radio drops significantly. Pilots or techs may perceive this as a malfunction — "my second radio keeps cutting out."
- **Root Cause:** This is an intentional feature called **"Monitored Com Muting."** It automatically reduces the volume of the non-selected COM to prevent it from competing with the active COM during critical communications.
- **Resolution:** This is configurable. To **toggle Monitored Com Muting on/off**, press and hold the COM1 or COM2 button until an aural confirmation message plays. Alternatively, an authorized Garmin service center can configure this setting via internal DIP switches accessible during bench setup.
- **Source:** Garmin GMA 345 Pilot's Guide; Garmin support documentation

### Monaural Headset in Stereo Jack = Left-Channel-Only Audio
- **Symptom:** A passenger or copilot using a monaural (single-plug) headset plugged into the GMA 345's stereo headset jacks hears audio only in the left ear. Additionally, *other* passengers with stereo headsets may also lose their right-channel audio.
- **Root Cause:** This is a wiring behavior, not a malfunction. A monaural headset plug shorts the right headset channel output to ground when inserted into a stereo jack. This does not damage the unit or headsets, but it routes all audio to the left channel only. Because the GMA 345's headset outputs share a common ground path, a monaural plug in one jack can affect stereo output to other jacks.
- **Resolution:** Use stereo headsets in all positions. If the aircraft has monaural headset jacks from a legacy installation, upgrading the jacks to stereo is required to benefit from 3D Audio and prevent this cross-talk issue.
- **Source:** Garmin GMA 345 documentation; Aviation Consumer review

### 3D Audio Conflicts With Lightspeed Zulu 3 Headsets
- **Symptom:** The 3D Audio spatial separation effect does not work correctly, or audio sounds distorted, when using Lightspeed Zulu 3 ANR headsets.
- **Root Cause:** The Lightspeed Zulu 3 has its own internal audio processing that can interfere with the GMA 345's phase-shifting algorithms. Additionally, incorrect headset configuration (stereo/mono switch position) can produce unexpected results.
- **Resolution:** Verify the Zulu 3's internal stereo/mono switch is set to **stereo**. If the conflict persists, disable 3D Audio on the GMA 345 for the affected headset position. Some pilots report that toggling the Zulu 3's "StreamPort" Bluetooth feature off resolves the issue.
- **Source:** Pilot reports on BeechtTalk and aviation forums
